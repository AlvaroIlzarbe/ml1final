History

The origins of Pokémon trace back to Satoshi Tajiri's childhood in Machida, Tokyo, where he explored fields and ponds searching for insects and small creatures like tadpoles and crabs. His goal was to collect, study, and classify them in a notebook his parents had given him.

During his second year of secondary school, Tajiri developed an interest in arcade video games, especially after an arcade opened near his home. This passion continued during his studies at the Tokyo College of Technology in the 1980s, where he frequented arcades in the Shimokitazawa, Shinjuku, and Shibuya neighborhoods.

In March 1983, at the age of seventeen, he launched the first issue of a dōjinshi magazine called Game Freak. The title referenced his fascination with the American film Freaks (1932). The magazine, handwritten by Tajiri, featured video game reviews and guides and was priced at 200 yen.

Around that time, Tajiri met Tsunekazu Ishihara and manga artist Ken Sugimori, who contributed illustrations to subsequent editions of Game Freak. The magazine ceased publication by the end of the decade.

With the success of the NES console, Tajiri and Sugimori decided to innovate for the platform. Tajiri transformed Game Freak into a company and developed a puzzle game called Mendel Palace (known in Japan as Quinty), which was released in 1989 and achieved notable success, marking the company's beginnings.

The following year, they decided to create a game for the Game Boy. Inspired by the Game Link Cable, Tajiri envisioned a game where information could be transferred between consoles. Influenced by franchises like Final Fantasy and Dragon Quest, he created an RPG where monsters could evolve and be transported.

The project, initially named Capsule Monster, was sent to Nintendo. Tajiri provided the main concept, while Sugimori designed the monsters. They also received advice from Shigeru Miyamoto (creator of Mario Bros.) to improve the game.

The production lasted five years. During that time, the Game Boy's popularity waned due to a lack of new games, and Game Freak faced financial struggles. Eventually, the project was renamed Pocket Monsters and released in February 1996 with the Aka and Midori ("Red" and "Green") versions. Initially, it was not very successful, but after a year, it surpassed one million copies sold.

Seeing this, Nintendo decided to bring the series to the Western market. The name was shortened to Pokémon to avoid conflicts with a series called Monster in My Pocket. Pokémon Red and Blue became a hit in the United States, selling over 200,000 copies in their first week.

Important concepts in Pokemon

Pokémon are creatures inspired by real animals, insects, objects, plants, or mythical beings, featuring a Lamarckian evolution system.

Players take on the role of Pokémon Trainers and have three main objectives:

  • Completing the Pokédex by collecting all available species of Pokémon.
  • Training a team of powerful Pokémon to compete against other trainers.
  • Winning the Pokémon League and becoming the regional champion.

The themes of collecting, training, and battling appear in nearly all versions of the Pokémon franchise, including video games, anime, manga, and the collectible card game.

In the main series, players embark on a journey through a specific Pokémon world region, where they must face Gym Leaders, powerful trainers, to earn badges and continue progressing.

Once all the region’s badges are collected, the player can challenge the Pokémon League, facing off against the best trainers to earn the Champion title.

The games also feature secondary storylines where players confront antagonist teams involved in trafficking, exploiting, or abusing Pokémon. Additionally, players may uncover mysteries about legendary or mythical Pokémon.

Each new generation of Pokémon games introduces unique mechanics and elements, evolving with each new installment.

Representative elements of the games

  • The figure of the Pokémon Trainer
    • The users of the video games are called Pokémon Trainers. They have two main goals (in most of the Pokémon games):
    1. Capture all available Pokémon species in the fictional region where the game is set, and thus complete the information for all Pokémon in the Pokédex.
    2. On the other hand, they must train them and battle other Pokémon belonging to other trainers to prove their skills, strength, talent, and become a Pokémon Master.
    1. To achieve this, Pokémon trainers travel across the regions of the Pokémon world, collecting gym badges that are earned after defeating the respective gym leaders. In these battles, both the trainer and the gym leader face their Pokémon to test their special abilities in a Pokémon battle
  • Types of Pokémon
    • There are 18 types that categorize the Pokémon introduced throughout the history of the games.
    1. Steel and Dark types were added in 2000 in the Gold, Silver, and Crystal games.
    2. The Fairy type was added in 2013 in the Pokémon X and Y games.
    3. A new pseudo-type introduced in the latest generations (Scarlet and Violet) is the Tera type. This type is only available and active through Terastallization, which is the unique game mechanic.
    • Each Pokémon has a set number of moves or attacks they can learn, which changes from one generation to another. These can be classified by their type or element (for example, the Thunder attack is an Electric-type attack).
    • Pokémon are also organized and divided this way, giving them advantages or disadvantages against others. This phenomenon is commonly known as the type chart, a representative table where we find 5 values:
    1. Immune: not affected by the attack.
    2. Not very effective: represented with damage values of 1/2 and 1/4; the damage value is calculated by multiplying it by 1/2 or 1/4.
    3. Neutral: represented with a damage value of x1; no additional calculations are applied to the damage.
    4. Effective: represented by a x2; a x2 multiplier is applied to the damage the attack does.
    5. Super effective: represented by a x4; a x4 multiplier is applied to the damage the attack does.

Starter Pokemon

One of the constant aspects of the Pokémon video games since their beginnings is that when the game starts, players receive their first Pokémon as a gift from the Local Professor (this depends on the region in which the game is set), an expert character in Pokémon.

Trainers receive a starter Pokémon to begin their journey in the game. The established number of starter Pokémon is three, from which trainers can choose only one. They are always of the water, fire, and grass types. (One Pokémon per type).

Another constant aspect is that the player's rival, a character who also becomes a trainer alongside the player, will always choose a Pokémon that has an advantage over the protagonist's Pokémon, except in Generations 7 and 8, where they choose the one that has a disadvantage.

In other games of the franchise that are not part of the main series, with the main series referring to the generational games that will be indicated later, the selection of starter Pokémon is different and they experimented with different styles and options.

Pokedex

It is an electronic device that appears in video games and the anime series. In the games, its function is to record data about a Pokémon. In the manga and anime, the Pokédex is an electronic encyclopedia that provides the trainer with information about an unknown Pokémon simply by pointing the Pokédex at the Pokémon.

In the video games, a trainer receives a blank Pokédex at the start of their journey. The goal is to complete the information about all the Pokémon available in the region where the game takes place. The trainer will receive the name and image of the Pokémon they have encountered.

In Pokémon Red and Blue, the Pokédex records information about each Pokémon simply by seeing it. More detailed information about a Pokémon becomes available after the trainer captures a wild Pokémon. This information includes the type, height, weight, techniques, and a brief description of the Pokémon. Current versions of the Pokédex contain comprehensive information about all known Pokémon.

In the GameCube games Pokémon Colosseum and Pokémon XD: Gale of Darkness, the Pokémon Digital Assistant (PDA) is used, which functions similarly to the Pokédex.

In the Alola region, the Pokédex is different from those in other regions. This version is embedded into a Rotom, a Pokémon that has the ability to inhabit appliances and other electronic devices. This Pokédex features cutting-edge technology from the Alola region, as it not only records entries for new Pokémon but also communicates and interacts with the player, takes photos, and acts as a map to show the player's exact location.

Anime

It is a metaserial anime created by Satoshi Tajiri, Junichi Masuda, and Ken Sugimori, narrating the story of Ash Ketchum, a young Pokémon Trainer from Pallet Town who embarks on a journey to achieve his dream of becoming a Pokémon Master.

The series is based on the Pokémon video game saga also created by Satoshi Tajiri, developed by Game Freak, and distributed by Nintendo. The games first appeared on the Japanese market on February 27, 1996.

The success of the video games, which continues to this day, led to their storyline being adapted into an anime produced by the animation studio OLM, Inc. The anime has been broadcast by the TV Tokyo network since its premiere on April 1, 1997.

The production of the anime includes a total of 27 seasons, each with a significant number of episodes, collectively exceeding 1,300:

These seasons follow the story of the main character, Ash Ketchum, a young trainer whose dream is to become a Pokémon Master. Accompanied by his inseparable Pikachu and friends like Misty and Brock, he travels through various regions, facing Gym Leaders, participating in tournaments, and battling villains like Team Rocket

Along his journey, Ash captures and trains Pokémon while exploring a world full of fantastic creatures, challenges, and lessons about friendship, perseverance, and teamwork. The series blends action, humor, and life lessons in a continuous quest to achieve his goals.

In 2023, a new series called Pokémon Horizons was released, introducing new protagonists such as Liko and Roy.

OLM, Inc. has produced 23 Pokémon movies to date.

Manga

There is a large number of Pokémon manga series. The mangas are usually released in Japan by Shōgakukan, and are translated into English in North America primarily by VIZ Media, and in Asian countries by Chuang Yi.

To date, more than 13 Pokémon sagas have been released. Some of these mangas are based on the anime and movies, while others are adaptations of the games and the Pokémon Trading Card Game.

Trading Card Game

The Trading Card Game, commonly abbreviated as TCG (from its English acronym), is a game developed by Pokémon and Nintendo.

It was released on October 20, 1996, in Japan by Media Factory.

Currently, there are approximately 13,605 cards, including all the expansions released over the years.

Basic Rules:

  • Two players face off with a deck of 60 cards.
  • There are four types of cards: Pokémon, Energy, Items, and Trainers.
  • Each player starts the game with 7 cards in hand. If a player does not have a Basic Pokémon (the first form in the evolutionary line), they place the cards back in the deck, shuffle it, and draw 7 new cards. Every time this happens, the opponent draws 1 additional card.
  • After placing the Pokémon on the board (called the "Active Pokémon"), 6 "Prize" cards are placed face-down.
  • Each time a Pokémon is knocked out (when its HP drops to zero), the player takes one of these "Prize" cards.
  • The first player to collect all 6 Prize cards wins.
